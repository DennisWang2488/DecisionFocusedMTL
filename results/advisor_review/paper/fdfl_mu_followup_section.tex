\subsection{Empirical Follow-Up: Fixed Prediction Weights and Normalized PCGrad}
\label{sec:exp-mu-followup}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
The main experiments revealed a sharp contrast between the healthcare and
knapsack settings. On healthcare, end-to-end methods with analytic
decision gradients were consistently stable, and the best scalarized and
multi-objective variants were tightly clustered. On the synthetic
multidimensional knapsack task, by contrast, the fair decision-focused
methods were much more sensitive to the decision-gradient approximation.
Two follow-up hypotheses emerged from the gradient logs. First, the poor
behavior of FDFL with $\mu=0$ could be caused by the absence of a
prediction anchor: when the decision gradient is estimated by SPSA, pure
decision-plus-fairness updates may allow the predictor to drift. Second,
PCGrad may be handicapped when one objective gradient is several orders
of magnitude larger than the others, so per-objective normalization before
projection may be necessary.

\paragraph{Follow-up design.}
We therefore ran two additional experiments. The first is an MD-knapsack
``$\mu$-sweep'' on the redesigned per-individual, two-resource knapsack
task with group-level $\alpha$-fair welfare, using $\alpha=2$,
fairness type MAD, $n_{\mathrm{train}}=300$, 30 training steps per
$\lambda$, 5 random seeds, and SPSA decision gradients with 8 directions
and perturbation scale $5\times 10^{-3}$. We compare the original FDFL
($\mu=0$), two fixed-anchor variants FDFL-0.1 and FDFL-0.5, the standard
scalarized method FDFL-Scal ($\mu=1$), FPLG, PCGrad with per-objective
gradient normalization, and the baseline methods FPTO, SAA, WDRO, and
MGDA. The second is a healthcare append run on the full Obermeyer cohort
with analytic decision gradients, which augments the existing grid with
the same fixed-$\mu$ variants while keeping the original
\texttt{stage\_results.csv} files untouched. This append run spans four
prediction-fairness measures, two welfare curvatures
$\alpha\in\{0.5,2\}$, 5 seeds, and 70 training steps. In both
experiments, performance is summarized by normalized test regret.

\paragraph{Results on MD knapsack.}
The $\mu$-sweep confirms that the no-anchor variant is the unstable one.
In Table~\ref{tab:mu-followup-summary}, FDFL at $\mu=0$ reaches
normalized regret $0.8384 \pm 0.3560$, far above every anchored
alternative and with very large seed variance. Introducing even a small
fixed prediction weight is enough to restore stability:
FDFL-0.1 drops to $0.1970 \pm 0.0037$, and FDFL-0.5 further improves to
$0.1864 \pm 0.0045$. These values place the fixed-$\mu$ variants in the
same performance band as FDFL-Scal ($0.1748 \pm 0.0069$) and FPLG
($0.1914 \pm 0.0041$). The interpretation is direct: under SPSA, the
decision gradient alone is too noisy to keep the predictor calibrated,
whereas even a weak MSE anchor prevents the runaway behavior observed for
$\mu=0$.

The normalized PCGrad variant does not recover the same benefit on this
task. Although normalization prevents the most extreme scale domination,
PCGrad still attains $0.3545 \pm 0.3618$, well above the scalarized
anchored methods and again with large variability. In this SPSA regime,
the main issue is not only objective-scale imbalance but also the
combination of noisy decision gradients and projection-based gradient
manipulation. The follow-up therefore refines the earlier interpretation:
adding a fixed prediction anchor is sufficient to stabilize scalarized
FDFL, but PCGrad remains brittle when conflict resolution is driven by
approximate decision gradients.

\paragraph{Results on healthcare.}
The healthcare append experiment tells the complementary story. Here the
decision gradient is analytic and low-variance, so changing $\mu$ has
almost no effect on regret. Across the four fixed-weight variants, the
MAD/$\alpha=2$ regret values are nearly indistinguishable:
FDFL = $0.1025 \pm 0.0270$, FDFL-0.1 = $0.1018 \pm 0.0268$,
FDFL-0.5 = $0.1023 \pm 0.0275$, and FDFL-Scal = $0.1028 \pm 0.0282$.
FPLG is likewise in the same band at $0.1017 \pm 0.0267$. In other
words, once the downstream gradient is reliable, the prediction anchor is
no longer the determining factor.

Normalized PCGrad behaves very differently in this analytic setting. It
achieves the best mean regret in the healthcare follow-up,
$0.0945 \pm 0.0424$, outperforming the scalarized fixed-$\mu$ family by
roughly $0.007$--$0.008$ in absolute normalized regret. This is
consistent with the intended effect of normalization: when the objective
gradients are informative but live on very different raw scales,
equalizing them before projection lets PCGrad exploit directional
conflicts without letting one objective dominate purely by magnitude.

\paragraph{Gradient-scale diagnostic.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gradient-scale-mu-followup} visualizes the mechanism
behind these contrasting outcomes. The top row (MD knapsack) shows a
large separation between decision-gradient and prediction-gradient norms,
especially for $\mu=0$, together with substantial variation over the
training trajectory. The bottom row (healthcare) is much flatter: the
gradient scales remain stable across $\mu$, and there is no comparable
explosion of the decision term. The figure reinforces the main empirical
message of the follow-up: fixed prediction weights matter when the
decision gradient is noisy and poorly scaled, but they have little effect
when the gradient is analytic and well behaved.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
Taken together, the two follow-up experiments isolate two distinct
failure modes. On SPSA-based tasks, the dominant issue is the absence of
an explicit prediction anchor: fixed $\mu>0$ stabilizes FDFL even when
the fairness term is present. On analytic-gradient tasks, that anchor is
largely irrelevant, but per-objective normalization improves PCGrad by
preventing raw gradient magnitude from overwhelming the projection
geometry. This suggests a practical rule: when decision gradients are
approximate, a fixed prediction-loss anchor is the first stabilizer to
add; when decision gradients are reliable but objectives differ strongly
in scale, normalized multi-objective projection is the more useful
correction.
